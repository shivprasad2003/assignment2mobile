Swami Vivekananda (1863–1902) was a prominent Indian monk, spiritual leader, and philosopher who played a pivotal role in introducing Hindu philosophy to the Western world. Born as Narendranath Datta in Calcutta (now Kolkata), he was drawn to spirituality from a young age. At 18, he met his spiritual mentor, Sri Ramakrishna Paramahamsa, and became a devoted disciple. After Ramakrishna's passing, Vivekananda embarked on a journey across India as a wandering monk, gaining firsthand insight into the country's social and economic challenges.

In 1893, Vivekananda gained international recognition by representing Hinduism at the Parliament of the World's Religions in Chicago. His opening words, "Sisters and brothers of America," resonated deeply with the audience, marking a significant moment in interfaith dialogue. He emphasized the universality of religions and the importance of tolerance and acceptance.

Following the Parliament, Vivekananda delivered numerous lectures across the United States and Europe, promoting the teachings of Vedanta and Yoga. He founded the Vedanta Society of New York and the Vedanta Society of San Francisco, establishing a foundation for Vedanta teachings in the West. In India, he established the Ramakrishna Math and the Ramakrishna Mission, organizations dedicated to spiritual training and social service.

Vivekananda is celebrated as a key figure in the Hindu Renaissance and a catalyst for social reform in colonial India. His teachings continue to inspire individuals worldwide, emphasizing self-realization, the unity of all religions, and the importance of serving humanity. In India, his birthday, January 12, is observed as National Youth Day, honoring his contributions to youth empowerment and national development.
